☉ History

Mexico City sits in a high basin once filled by Lake Texcoco and its sister lakes. The Mexica built Tenochtitlan on an island and linked it to the shores by long causeways.

Chinampas - rectangular garden beds built of reeds, mud, and vegetation - expanded farmland and created a canal network that functioned like streets. Canoes moved people, food, and goods through the city.

After the conquest, Spanish engineers and later governments drained the lakes to control flooding, a centuries-long project that reshaped the valley. The city spread outward as water retreated.

That drainage left soft lakebed soils. Today the city slowly subsides, and historic buildings tilt or crack as the ground shifts beneath them.

The Aztec Empire

+

The Mexica people founded Tenochtitlan in 1325 on an island in Lake Texcoco, fulfilling a prophecy that they should build where they saw an eagle perched on a cactus devouring a serpent â€” an image that still adorns the Mexican flag. By the early 16th century, Tenochtitlan had become one of the largest cities in the world, with over 200,000 inhabitants, rivaling Constantinople and Paris.

The city was an engineering marvel. Three great causeways connected the island to the mainland. Aqueducts carried fresh water from Chapultepec springs. The chinampas â€” floating gardens built on the lake â€” fed the population with maize, beans, squash, chili peppers, tomatoes, and amaranth. A complex system of canals served as streets, making Tenochtitlan a kind of American Venice.

The Great Temple (Templo Mayor) stood at the heart of the city â€” a twin pyramid dedicated to Huitzilopochtli, god of war and the sun, and Tlaloc, god of rain and agriculture. The temple was rebuilt seven times, each version larger than the last, its summit visible across the entire valley. Surrounding it were dozens of smaller temples, a ball court, a skull rack (tzompantli), and the palaces of the tlatoani (ruler).

Aztec society was rigidly stratified. At the top sat the tlatoani, believed to be a representative of the gods. Below were the pipiltin (nobles), then the macehualtin (commoners), and the tlacotin (slaves). Warriors who captured enemies in battle could rise in status, earning the right to wear jaguar skins or eagle feathers. Education was compulsory: boys attended either the telpochcalli (commoner school) or the calmecac (noble school), while girls learned domestic arts and could serve as priestesses. The market at Tlatelolco, which we'll visit on Day 3, was the largest in the Americas â€” hosting 60,000 traders daily.
